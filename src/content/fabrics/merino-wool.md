Merino wool is the starting point for most serious conversations about natural-fiber performance. The fiber comes from Merino sheep bred primarily in Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa, and the defining characteristic is diameter: finer micron counts produce softer, more functional cloth.

## Micron and the Itch Threshold

The itch response in wool is mechanical — coarser fibers poke against skin receptors. The threshold for most people is around 20 microns; anything above that will feel prickly worn next to skin. Ultrafine Merino sits at 15.5 microns or below, which puts it in cashmere territory for softness. Outlier's Ultrafine grade, for example, uses 16.5μ fiber and produces a fabric that reads almost like a fine jersey knit.

Brands like Icebreaker, Wool&Prince, and Smartwool work in the 17–19μ range — still soft enough for most, and more durable than the finest grades.

## Why It Works

Merino's moisture management is structural, not chemical. Each fiber has a hydrophilic core that absorbs up to 30% of its weight in vapor without feeling wet, and a hydrophobic outer scale that repels liquid. The result is active moisture transport: sweat moves from skin to fabric to air without the clammy feedback of synthetics.

Odor resistance is similarly structural. The protein structure of wool binds the volatile sulfur compounds produced by bacteria rather than trapping them in the fiber matrix. This is why a well-worn Merino shirt can go three days without washing where a polyester equivalent can't manage one.

## Temperature Regulation

Merino's crimp — the natural waviness of the fiber — creates millions of tiny air pockets throughout the fabric. These act as insulation when still, and the fiber's high moisture absorption delays the cooling effect of sweat evaporation. The result is a fabric that buffers temperature swings in both directions. It is genuinely multi-season in a way synthetics rarely achieve.

## Construction and Weight

Knit construction matters as much as fiber quality. A fine-gauge jersey (like most base-layer tees) prioritizes softness and next-to-skin comfort. A rib or waffle knit adds stretch and surface area. A boucle or terry loop construction adds loft for mid-layer warmth. 

Weight in GSM is the primary lever for application:
- **150–175 GSM** — ultralight, hot-weather or extreme-activity base layers  
- **185–220 GSM** — the most versatile range; year-round base layer or light overshirt  
- **250–400 GSM** — mid-layer weights; shirts worn as outerwear, or sweaters

## Durability

The main honest criticism of fine Merino is durability. High micron counts increase abrasion resistance — finer fiber means fewer scales per surface area and more susceptibility to pilling and wear at friction points. Blending with nylon (5–20%) at seams or throughout dramatically extends garment life without meaningfully compromising feel. Outlier blends with Tencel in some constructions; Icebreaker uses Corespun nylon-core yarn as their primary durability solution.

## Key Brands and Constructions

- **Outlier** — Ultrafine grade (16.5μ), made in Thailand, focused on minimal construction and maximum hand-feel  
- **Wool&Prince** — 17.5μ, popular shirt formats, good balance of softness and durability  
- **Icebreaker** — ZQ-certified NZ Merino, 150–260 GSM range, Corespun for durability  
- **Smartwool** — broad range, 150–400 GSM, performance focus, reliable durability  
- **Nanamica** — uses Merino in more tailored formats; mid-layer overshirts and dress applications
